## Building Standards

<Callout type="info">
The core of M01 is "curiosity is the most precious ability." Assessment criteria are designed around **sparking, protecting, and spreading curiosity**, so that the assessment itself embodies the spirit of curiosity.
</Callout>

### Core Principles

| ID | Core Principle | One-Liner | Practice Signals |
|----|---------------|-----------|-----------------|
| **N1** | Questions First | Great questions are more precious than great answers | Encourage questioning; don't rush to provide answers |
| **N2** | Learning by Doing | Create external artifacts to construct internal understanding | Hands-on time >= lecture time |
| **N3** | Failure is a Gift | Every failure signals a knowledge gap | Celebrate valuable failures |
| **N4** | Playful Spirit | Explore with a playful mindset to reduce fear of mistakes | Allow "useless" exploration |
| **N5** | Community Ignition | Curiosity is contagious — it amplifies within communities | Share discoveries; inspire each other |

### Mandatory Standards (10 items)

| ID | Standard | Why It Matters | Quantitative Metric | Tier |
|----|----------|---------------|---------------------|------|
| **S1** | Active Question Wall | Curiosity manifests through questions | Collect >= 20 learner questions per month | Foundation |
| **S2** | "I Don't Know" Moments | Mentors model a knowledge-seeking attitude | Mentors record >= 3 "let's explore together" instances per month | Foundation |
| **S3** | Failure Story Library | Transform failure into learning resources | Accumulate >= 10 documented failure cases | Foundation |
| **S4** | Free Exploration Time | Curiosity needs uninterrupted time | >= 2 hours of open exploration per week | Foundation |
| **S5** | Unsolved Mysteries Display | Ignite curiosity with open questions | Display >= 5 open questions in the space | Foundation |
| **S6** | Visible Principles | Let principles permeate daily life | Core principles visible in >= 3 locations | Foundation |
| **S7** | Surprise Discovery Log | Capture "Aha!" moments | Record >= 5 unexpected discoveries per month | Growth |
| **S8** | Best Question Awards | Reinforce "questions matter more than answers" | Select >= 1 "Curiosity Star" per month | Growth |
| **S9** | Cross-Disciplinary Collisions | Innovation comes from unexpected connections | >= 1 cross-disciplinary surprise case per quarter | Excellence |
| **S10** | Principles as Stories | Convey through stories, not lectures | >= 5 principle stories circulating in the community | Excellence |

### Parameter Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Calculation | Benchmark |
|--------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Questioning Density** | Frequency of learner-initiated questions | Total monthly questions / Active learners | >= 3 per person per month |
| **Exploration Time Ratio** | Free exploration as proportion of total learning time | Free exploration time / Total learning time x 100% | >= 20% |
| **Failure Conversion Rate** | Proportion of failures documented and shared | Documented failures / Total setbacks x 100% | >= 30% |

### Extension Patterns

| Pattern | Description | Applicable Context |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Night Science Workshops** | Inspired by the "night science" concept — create space for messy, intuitive, trial-and-error exploration without demanding immediate results | Labs pursuing deep innovation |
| **Curiosity Contagion Program** | Regularly invite "intensely curious" external figures (scientists, artists, entrepreneurs) to share their questions and puzzles | Labs needing fresh perspectives |

## Introduction: Why We Must Question the Fundamentals of Education

When we talk about laboratories, makerspaces, and innovative education, we often fall into a technocentric illusion — focusing on what equipment to configure, what courses to design, and what tools to adopt. Yet behind these operational questions lie more fundamental inquiries: Why do we need laboratories? How does genuine learning happen? What kind of education can cultivate people ready for the future?

OWL was founded through the sustained exploration of these fundamental questions. We deeply believe that education is not pouring water into empty bottles, but lighting a fire. Behind this metaphor, attributed to Yeats, lies more than half a century of learning science research. Piaget discovered through observing how children interact with blocks, sand, and water that learners are not passive receivers of knowledge but active constructors of meaning. Vygotsky emphasized the critical role of social interaction — his concept of the "zone of proximal development" explains why appropriate challenge combined with proper support produces optimal learning outcomes. Papert at MIT took these insights to new heights with constructionism: when learners construct internal understanding by creating external artifacts — whether Logo programs or LEGO robots — learning is most profound and lasting.

In today's era of rapidly advancing artificial intelligence, these ideas are not just still relevant — they are more important than ever. When AI can generate code, design drawings, and write reports, the unique human value lies in asking meaningful questions, making responsible judgments, and creating emotionally rich works. OWL is dedicated to cultivating these irreplaceable capabilities — critical thinking, creativity, collaboration, and ethical judgment.

This section will systematically articulate the theoretical foundations and value system of OWL laboratories. Starting from core principles, we will progressively explore the mission and vision, core values, design principles, theoretical frameworks, 21st-century competency frameworks, and the trajectory of the innovative education movement. This theoretical system is not a castle in the air built from nothing, but is deeply rooted in learning science research and global maker education practice. It will provide a solid conceptual foundation for all subsequent modules, ensuring that every decision and practice at OWL is evidence-based and principled.

## The Fundamental Transformation of Education

Education is undergoing a paradigm-level restructuring — transitioning from the industrial-age model to a new learning ecosystem suited for the AI era. The following five shifts are prerequisites for understanding all OWL principles and practices.

### Competency Orientation

> When AI can pass all standardized tests, what are we still testing for?

**Core Shift**: From test scores to truly important capabilities: independent thinking, creating new things, effective communication, team collaboration

**OWL Practice**:
- Multi-dimensional assessment: project portfolios, process documentation, peer feedback
- Real-problem driven: learning to solve real-world problems

### Thinking Cultivation

> When Google can deliver any fact in 0.5 seconds, what is the value of memorizing knowledge?

**Core Shift**: From knowledge memorization to metacognition (learning how to learn)

**OWL Practice**:
- Making thinking visible: whiteboards and mind maps externalize thought processes
- Socratic dialogue: mentors don't give answers — they ask "why do you think that?"
- Reflection journals: documenting changes in thinking and improvement strategies

### Personalization

> Innovation never comes from averages — Einstein was considered "slow" in school, and Jobs dropped out yet changed the world.

**Core Shift**: From standardized assembly lines to recognizing every student as unique

**OWL Practice**:
- Diverse learning zones: quiet reading corners, collaboration areas, making spaces, coding areas
- "Low floors, high ceilings, wide walls": multiple entry points, multiple paths, multiple exits
- Personalized learning plans: mentors and students co-design goals

### Student-Centered

> You cannot learn to swim by watching videos of other people swimming.

**Core Shift**: From teacher lecturing to student active construction

**OWL Practice**:
- Flexible, reconfigurable spaces: furniture changes with project needs
- Project-driven learning: questions emerge from student curiosity
- Just-enough help: don't give answers, but provide just-enough hints when students are stuck

### Open Ecosystem

> Real problems aren't in textbooks, and real mentors aren't only in schools.

**Core Shift**: From closed silos to a learning ecosystem connected to the real world

**OWL Practice**:
- Open physical space: community members can visit, use, and share
- Open Educational Resources (OER): students are both users and creators
- Community mentor networks: engineers, artists, and scientists guide together

## Core Principles — Action Guide

Core principles are OWL laboratory's action guide. They originate from learning science research and practical experience, running through every aspect of laboratory construction and operations. These principles are not abstract slogans but compasses that guide direction in concrete decisions. When a mentor faces the dilemma of "should I tell the learner the answer," the "empower, not replace" principle reminds her: first assess the learner's current capability boundary, provide just-enough scaffolding, and let them find the answer themselves. When a curriculum designer struggles with "who decides the project topic," the "interest-driven" principle provides direction: start from what learners care about, even if it doesn't look very "academic."

OWL's core principle system encompasses six interrelated dimensions that together form the fundamental framework for laboratory action.

### Learning by Doing: Practice is the Heart of Learning

This principle originates from Dewey's pragmatist educational philosophy, emphasizing that learning is acquired through hands-on operation and experience. Dewey systematically articulated the "learning by doing" philosophy in his classic work *Democracy and Education*, arguing that traditional education overemphasizes passive reception of knowledge while neglecting learners' active inquiry and practical operation.

In OWL laboratories, the learning-by-doing principle is fully implemented. Every learning project ensures that hands-on practice time accounts for no less than 60%, with learners constructing knowledge through making, debugging, and improving artifacts rather than passively receiving instruction.

### Open Sharing: Knowledge Flows Freely

Knowledge is the common wealth of humanity and should not be privatized or enclosed. This principle is deeply rooted in the cultural DNA of the open-source movement. OWL laboratories view open sharing as a core value, encouraging learners to share their project outcomes through open-source channels and using Creative Commons or similar open licenses to promote the free flow of knowledge within the community.

### Community-Driven: Learning Happens in Communities

Learning is a social behavior. Think about how a tailor's apprentice grows: they start by threading needles and organizing fabric, observing the master's cutting techniques; gradually they take on more complex tasks until they can work independently. OWL laboratories' community design follows the same logic: newcomers start with simple tasks and progressively grow into experts who can guide others through authentic collaboration.

### Failure-Friendly: Trial and Error is the Path of Learning

In the innovation process, failure is the norm and an important learning opportunity. Any major innovation is accompanied by countless failures and iterations, just as Thomas Edison said — he didn't fail ten thousand times, but discovered ten thousand ways that didn't work. Viewing failure as a necessary part of the learning journey rather than an obstacle to avoid is an essential prerequisite for cultivating innovation capability.

### Cross-Disciplinary Integration: Breaking Subject Boundaries

Real-world problems rarely confine themselves to a single discipline. Climate change, public health, urban planning — virtually all major social challenges require the knowledge and methods of multiple disciplines working together. OWL laboratory learning experiences break down subject boundaries, organically integrating knowledge from science, technology, engineering, arts, and mathematics to cultivate comprehensive literacy.

### Empowerment, Not Replacement: Technology and Tools Empower People

Technology is a tool, not an end. In today's era of rapidly advancing artificial intelligence, this principle is especially important. When AI can generate code, design drawings, and write reports, the unique human value lies in asking meaningful questions, making responsible judgments, and creating emotionally rich works. OWL laboratories emphasize human-AI collaboration, where technological tools assist creation and learning rather than replacing human creativity and critical thinking.
